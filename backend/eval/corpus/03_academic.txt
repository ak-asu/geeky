Bayesian Knowledge Tracing: Modeling Student Learning in Intelligent Tutoring Systems

## Abstract

Bayesian Knowledge Tracing (BKT) is a statistical method for estimating student knowledge states from sequences of observed practice attempts. Introduced by Corbett and Anderson in 1995, BKT models each skill as a binary latent variable — either known or not known — and updates the probability of mastery after each student response using Bayes' theorem. Despite its simplicity relative to later approaches, BKT remains one of the most widely used and studied models in educational data mining. This article reviews the theoretical foundations of BKT, its parameterization, training methodology, extensions, known limitations, and current standing against competing models.

## 1. Introduction and Motivation

Intelligent Tutoring Systems (ITS) aim to provide personalized instruction by adapting the content, difficulty, and pacing of material to individual students. Central to this adaptation is a student model — a computational representation of what the student currently knows. Without an accurate student model, an ITS cannot make informed decisions about when to advance to new material, when to provide remediation, or when to offer a hint rather than simply waiting.

Before BKT, early student models relied on simple counts of correct responses or rule-based representations. These approaches treated observable responses as direct readouts of knowledge, which is problematic for two reasons. First, a student might answer correctly by guessing, inflating the apparent knowledge estimate. Second, a student might slip and answer incorrectly despite knowing the relevant skill, deflating the estimate. Both noise sources cause systematic miscalibration that worsens over time.

BKT addressed this by modeling two distinct sources of response noise: slip (answering incorrectly despite knowing the skill) and guess (answering correctly without knowing the skill). By separating knowledge from performance, BKT provides a more principled basis for tracking learning progress.

## 2. Theoretical Foundations

### 2.1 Hidden Markov Model Structure

BKT is formally a hidden Markov model (HMM) with a binary latent state. The latent state $K_t$ represents whether the student has acquired the target skill at time $t$. The state is hidden — the tutor observes only whether each response was correct or incorrect, not the underlying knowledge state.

The model has four parameters:

- $p_L$: Prior probability that the skill is known before instruction begins.
- $p_T$: Probability that an unknown skill becomes known after a single practice opportunity (the transition or learning rate).
- $p_G$: Probability of a correct response given that the skill is not known (guess probability).
- $p_S$: Probability of an incorrect response given that the skill is known (slip probability).

BKT assumes knowledge acquisition is permanent: once $K_t = 1$, it remains 1 for all subsequent steps. This is a strong simplification — empirical research on the forgetting curve shows memory decays in the absence of review — but it is tractable and performs reasonably well on short-horizon prediction tasks where the practice session occurs within a single day.

### 2.2 Bayesian Updating Equations

Given an observed correct response at step $t$, BKT updates the posterior probability of knowledge:

$$P(K_t | \text{correct}) = \frac{(1 - p_S) \cdot P(K_t)}{(1 - p_S) \cdot P(K_t) + p_G \cdot (1 - P(K_t))}$$

And for an incorrect response:

$$P(K_t | \text{incorrect}) = \frac{p_S \cdot P(K_t)}{p_S \cdot P(K_t) + (1 - p_G) \cdot (1 - P(K_t))}$$

After the observation update, the transition probability is applied to account for possible learning during this step:

$$P(K_{t+1}) = P(K_t | E_{1:t}) + (1 - P(K_t | E_{1:t})) \cdot p_T$$

This recursive update is applied after every response, producing a time-series estimate of the student's mastery probability. The update is computationally cheap — $O(1)$ per response — making BKT suitable for real-time use in deployed tutoring systems.

### 2.3 Mastery Thresholding

A student is considered to have mastered a skill when $P(K_t)$ exceeds a predefined threshold, typically set at 0.95 in deployed systems. Once mastery is declared, the system advances the student to new material. The threshold choice involves a real tradeoff: a higher threshold of 0.95 reduces false declarations of mastery but increases the review burden, sometimes causing students to practice a skill far beyond the point where additional practice is beneficial. A lower threshold, such as 0.80, reduces unnecessary practice but risks advancing students before they are genuinely ready, which can compound as prerequisite gaps accumulate.

## 3. Parameter Estimation

### 3.1 Maximum Likelihood Estimation via EM

BKT parameters are typically estimated from historical datasets of student performance using maximum likelihood estimation (MLE). Because the latent state sequence is unobserved, direct MLE is intractable; the Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm is used instead.

The E-step applies the forward-backward algorithm to compute the posterior probability of the latent state at each time step given all observations and the current parameter estimates. The M-step updates parameters to maximize the expected complete-data log-likelihood under those posteriors. Iterations continue until the log-likelihood improvement falls below a convergence threshold.

In practice, BKT is fit independently for each skill using all available student response sequences for that skill. Fitting is generally fast: even with thousands of students and hundreds of practice opportunities per skill, convergence typically occurs within 20 to 50 EM iterations.

### 3.2 Identifiability and Constraints

A well-known challenge in BKT is parameter identifiability. Multiple parameter configurations can produce equivalent fits to a given dataset. The degeneracy is most severe when guess and slip probabilities are large, as the model can then attribute almost any response pattern to performance noise rather than to changes in the latent knowledge state. Standard constraints applied during fitting include: $p_G < 0.5$ (a student should not guess correctly more than half the time on average), $p_S < 0.5$ (slipping should be exceptional rather than frequent), and $p_G + p_S < 1$ (the combined noise level should not overwhelm the knowledge signal).

Even with these constraints, different parameter initializations can converge to different local maxima. Multiple random restarts with the best-fit solution selected by log-likelihood are recommended in practice.

### 3.3 Student-Level vs. Population-Level Parameters

Standard BKT fits one set of parameters per skill across all students. This implicitly assumes uniform prior knowledge and learning rate across the student population, which is obviously false. Researchers have explored fitting per-student parameters — either allowing the prior $p_L$ to vary by student while holding $p_T$, $p_G$, $p_S$ at population levels, or fitting all four parameters per student.

Per-student parameterization improves predictive accuracy on held-out data but requires enough observations per student to estimate parameters reliably. In sparse data regimes, per-student fitting can overfit. A middle-ground approach clusters students into groups with similar learning profiles and fits group-level parameters, which has shown improvements over pure population-level fitting without the sample-size requirements of full per-student estimation.

## 4. Extensions of Standard BKT

### 4.1 Contextual BKT

Baker, Corbett, and Aleven (2008) introduced contextual BKT, extending the model to allow guess and slip probabilities to vary based on contextual features of the practice situation. Contextual features include item difficulty, student affect (detected from interaction patterns), and off-task behavior. For example, an item encountered by a student showing off-task behavior signals is assigned a higher guess probability, as the response is less likely to reflect genuine knowledge engagement.

In empirical evaluations on the Cognitive Tutor log dataset, contextual BKT showed improved predictive accuracy compared to standard BKT, particularly on items where contextual factors would be expected to have strong influence. The improvement in area under the ROC curve was approximately 0.03 to 0.05 AUC points, which is meaningful given the strong performance of standard BKT on these datasets.

### 4.2 BKT with Forgetting

Standard BKT assumes knowledge, once acquired, never decays. BKT-F adds a forgetting transition: the latent state can transition from known to unknown with some probability $p_F$ per time step. This requires one additional parameter and two-directional Bayes updates.

Empirical results on whether BKT-F improves predictive accuracy are mixed and depend heavily on the time granularity of the dataset. In tutoring system logs where all practice occurs within a single session, forgetting dynamics are largely irrelevant and BKT-F provides no benefit. In datasets spanning multiple sessions separated by days or weeks, BKT-F shows modest improvements, particularly for skills that were practiced early in the dataset and then left unreinforced for extended periods.

### 4.3 Deep Knowledge Tracing

Deep Knowledge Tracing (DKT), introduced by Piech et al. (2015), replaces the BKT framework entirely with a long short-term memory (LSTM) recurrent neural network. The network takes a sequence of (skill, correctness) pairs as input and predicts the probability of a correct response on the next item for every possible skill. DKT is trained end-to-end using cross-entropy loss.

In the original evaluation on two large benchmark datasets (ASSISTments 2009 and Khan Academy), DKT outperformed BKT by approximately 0.06 AUC points. Subsequent replications have shown more modest improvements of 0.01 to 0.03 AUC, with the gap depending substantially on how BKT is implemented and whether skill-level parameter fitting is done properly.

DKT implicitly models skill interdependencies that BKT treats as independent. A student who has mastered calculus will likely learn integration by parts faster, and DKT can learn this association from data without it being explicitly encoded. However, DKT provides no interpretable parameters analogous to BKT's $p_L$, $p_T$, $p_G$, $p_S$, making it less useful in contexts where educators want to understand what the model is claiming about a student's knowledge state.

### 4.4 Hybrid Approaches

Several recent architectures attempt to combine BKT's interpretability with the representational capacity of neural networks. One approach retains the four-parameter BKT structure but learns to predict parameters as functions of student and item features using neural networks. Another approach trains a neural feature extractor jointly with a BKT-style mastery tracking module, preserving interpretable mastery estimates while allowing rich feature representations. These hybrid models have shown state-of-the-art performance on several benchmarks while maintaining interpretable knowledge state estimates.

## 5. Practical Deployment

### 5.1 Carnegie Learning and Cognitive Tutor

Carnegie Learning's Cognitive Tutor, deployed in high school mathematics classrooms across the United States, uses BKT to track student mastery across hundreds of algebra and geometry sub-skills. A landmark randomized controlled trial by Koedinger et al. conducted in the early 2000s compared Cognitive Tutor with standard textbook instruction. Students in the Cognitive Tutor condition showed significantly higher performance on standardized assessments administered two years after the intervention, demonstrating durable learning gains attributable to the adaptive mastery tracking and personalized practice scheduling.

### 5.2 ASSISTments

ASSISTments is a free web-based mathematics tutoring platform developed at Worcester Polytechnic Institute and used in thousands of classrooms in the United States. It uses BKT for skill mastery tracking and has served as the source of multiple large-scale benchmark datasets that have driven educational data mining research. The ASSISTments 2009-2010 and 2012-2013 datasets are among the most widely cited benchmarks in the knowledge tracing literature.

## 6. Known Limitations

### 6.1 Binary Knowledge Assumption

The binary latent state — a student either knows a skill or does not — is a significant theoretical oversimplification. Knowledge acquisition in humans is gradual, context-dependent, and multidimensional. A student may demonstrate procedural fluency without conceptual understanding, or apply a skill correctly in familiar contexts but fail to transfer it to novel problems. The binary model cannot represent these distinctions.

### 6.2 Skill Independence

Standard BKT models each skill as statistically independent, with no mechanism for capturing prerequisite relationships or transfer effects. A student who has mastered arithmetic will typically learn elementary algebra faster, but BKT cannot exploit this. Graph-based extensions that encode prerequisite relationships and allow knowledge transfer across related skills have been proposed but are less commonly deployed due to the added complexity and data requirements.

### 6.3 Response Order Sensitivity

BKT parameter estimates depend on the order in which items are presented. An easy-to-hard ordering produces different parameter estimates than random ordering, even when the underlying true mastery trajectory is identical. This creates a feedback loop in adaptive systems: the order in which the system presents items affects the parameter estimates that guide future item selection.

## 7. Conclusion

Bayesian Knowledge Tracing remains a foundational model in intelligent tutoring and educational data mining. Its combination of interpretable parameters, principled Bayesian updates, and computational efficiency has sustained its relevance for three decades. While neural approaches such as DKT and its variants have demonstrated superior predictive accuracy on benchmark datasets, BKT's interpretability and its well-studied failure modes keep it practically valuable as both a baseline and as a component in hybrid architectures. The most productive research direction currently is combining BKT's knowledge-state interpretability with the representational richness of learned feature extractors, producing systems that can be both accurate and transparent to educators.
